# Case Study

Civil aviation flight safety has always been the top priority in the aviation sector. Currently, limited by capabilities in data collection, transmission, and real-time utilization, civil aviation safety management is still primarily based on post-event analysis. That is, airlines usually need to wait until a flight lands before they can systematically obtain time-series data of various critical flight parameters across the entire process from takeoff to landing. Therefore, how to utilize these data to conduct attribution analysis on risks generated during flight, and to provide guidance for future specific pilot operations, is a highly important research task.

Take aircraft "long landing" as an example. A long landing refers to an abnormal landing scenario where the actual touchdown point of the aircraft is significantly later than the standard touchdown zone after the runway threshold, thereby increasing the risk of running off the runway. Assume that you, as a data researcher at a civil aviation research institute, have access to all the flight data required for the study, including various flight parameters, flight environmental variables, and pilot operation parameters represented by "pitch stick input". Please start from data selection and construct a complete research scheme, ultimately achieving an analysis of a newly landed flight that experienced a "long landing" to determine the possible causes, and take "pitch stick input" as an example to give specific recommendations on how the pilot should adjust during the operation.

Please elaborate in detail on each step and key details of the research scheme, and demonstrate the rationality, feasibility, superiority, and expected effects of this scheme. At the same time, please analyze the potential problems and room for subsequent improvement of this scheme.
