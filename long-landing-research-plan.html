Section 1

Why long landings matter

Landing occupies about 1% of flight time yet produced 35% of fatal commercial-jet accidents worldwide in 2016–2025; adding final approach brings the share to 48%.[10] Runway excursions are the most common accident category of the past decade: 21% of all accidents in 2015–2024, of which 80% occurred during landing.[21] Within that category, the long (deep) landing is a named, measurable precursor. Boeing's analysis of landing-overrun events attributes approximately 44% of runway overrun excursions to long landings, and identifies the long landing as the primary contributing factor to an overrun in the window between decision height and touchdown.[11] An IATA five-year analysis found a long, floated, bounced, firm, off-center or crabbed landing was a factor in 43% of runway excursion accidents.[22]

Two accidents frame the risk chain. Air France 358 (Toronto, 2005) touched down about 3,800 ft down a 9,000 ft rain-soaked runway after crossing the threshold roughly 40 ft above glideslope with a tailwind building to 10 kt; reverse thrust came 12.8 s after touchdown, and the A340 departed the runway end at about 80 kt and burned.[20] Southwest 1248 (Chicago Midway, 2005) touched down inside the touchdown zone yet still overran because thrust reversers were not deployed until about 15 seconds after touchdown on a contaminated runway with an 8 kt tailwind — the accident that triggered the FAA's time-of-arrival landing assessment policy and the TALPA rulemaking.[7] The lesson for a research scheme: touchdown point is the single strongest lever the pilot controls in the air, but attribution must treat the landing as a chain — approach energy, threshold crossing, flare, touchdown, deceleration — because the same overrun outcome can be produced at different links.[8]

The gap this scheme fills. Current airline flight-data monitoring reliably detects the long-landing exceedance after the fact, but detection is a count, not an explanation. The research task set by the case study — attribute the cause for a specific newly landed flight and convert it into concrete pitch-stick input advice — requires a per-flight attribution layer on top of the exceedance engine. That layer is the core of this design.

Section 2

Operational definition and event standard

The scheme needs a definition that is computable from QAR data, defensible against certified performance assumptions, and aligned with what regulators already monitor.

2.1 What counts as the touchdown zone

  • FAA doctrine: the touchdown zone is 500–3,000 ft beyond the threshold, not to exceed the first one-third of the runway; the typical operational touchdown point is in the first third, with a target about 1,000 ft down the runway.[2]
  • Certified performance assumption: operational landing data assume an air distance of 1,500 ft or 7 seconds from threshold to touchdown; if the airplane touches down beyond the air distance included in the AFM landing distance, the calculated landing distance may not be achievable.[1] A minimum 15% safety margin on the at-arrival landing distance is FAA policy.[4]
  • European FDM practice: the EASA-hosted EOFDM forum defines the precursor "Deep Landing" (RE29) as AirDistance — distance from runway threshold crossing to the touchdown point — raised as an event when it exceeds a threshold, and "Long Flare" (RE28) as TimeFlare, the time from 50 ft above aerodrome level to touchdown.[14] EASA good practice further recommends decomposing "risk of runway overrun at landing" detections into "touchdown point far beyond the recommended touchdown zone" versus "too little/too late deceleration after touchdown".[13]

2.2 Event standard adopted by this scheme

Two computed measurements, each with a two-level threshold, per aircraft type and runway:

Long-landing event standard (prototype defaults, tuned per fleet in stage 4)
MeasurementDefinition (per EOFDM RE29 / RE28 [14])Level 1 — cautionLevel 2 — warning
air_distanceAlong-runway distance from threshold crossing (50 ft point) to main-gear touchdown> 750 m (≈ 2,460 ft)> 900 m (≈ 2,950 ft) or beyond first third of LDA
time_flareElapsed time from 50 ft AAL to weight-on-wheels> 9 s> 11 s

The Level 2 distance threshold sits at the boundary of the FAA touchdown-zone definition (3,000 ft cap)[2] and roughly twice the certified 1,500 ft air-distance allowance[1]; the time threshold reflects the certified 7 s allowance plus flare dispersion. Thresholds are deliberately expressed in both distance and time because the two catch different failure modes: a fast, shallow float trips distance before time; a slow, held-off flare trips time before distance. Chinese QAR practice uses the same two-measurement structure (Section 9).

Before airline QAR access is granted, the pipeline is developed against NASA's DASHlink sample flight data — real, de-identified recordings from a regional-jet fleet with 186 parameters per flight including control-column, radio-altitude and ground-speed channels — which is publicly released precisely for developing such safety data-mining methods.[25]

Section 4

Research scheme: the seven-stage pipeline

The scheme runs the same seven stages in two modes: batch mode over the historical fleet to learn what normal and long landings look like, and single-flight mode when a newly landed flight trips the event standard and needs an explanation.

Stage 1 — Ingest and decode

Raw QAR frames (ARINC 717/767) are decoded into engineering units against the fleet's data-frame definition, resampled onto a common clock, and stored as one table per flight. Every downstream artifact records the decoder version.

Stage 2 — Quality control

Range checks, spike and dropout repair, weight-on-wheels debounce, cross-channel consistency (e.g., radio altitude vs pressure altitude near ground). Flights failing QC are quarantined, never silently dropped: attrition itself is a monitored metric so the study population stays representative.

Stage 3 — Phase segmentation and geometry

Detects the segment boundaries the analysis hangs on: stabilization gate crossings (1,000 ft IMC / 500 ft VMC per the industry stabilized-approach standard[24]), threshold crossing, flare initiation (first sustained aft pitch-input above noise below 100 ft), touchdown, and computes air_distance by projecting GPS positions onto the runway centerline with a ground-speed-integration fallback (the EOFDM RE29 measurement[14]).

Stage 4 — Event labeling and threshold calibration

Applies the two-level standard of Section 2.2 to the full history; calibrates thresholds per type and airport so that Level 1 flags roughly the worst 2–3% of landings (monitoring yield) while Level 2 stays anchored to the physical limits. Produces the labeled study population.

Stage 5 — Feature engineering

Compresses each landing into interpretable features chosen to mirror the quantified factors in FAA and Flight Safety Foundation guidance, so that model attributions are directly comparable with published physics: threshold-crossing height and speed deviation, tailwind component at 50 ft, glideslope deviation history, flare-initiation height, pitch-input shape descriptors (peak, rate, reversal count), thrust-retard height, float time.[1][23]

Stage 6 — Dual-track modeling

Track A (population attribution): a gradient-boosted regression predicts air_distance from pre-touchdown features under monotonicity constraints that encode known physics (tailwind cannot reduce distance, higher threshold crossing cannot shorten it). SHAP values then decompose each flight's predicted excess into per-factor meter contributions — the same explainable-ML pattern already published for QAR exceedance analysis.[27] Track B (operation-profile comparison): the pitch-stick trace of the flight is compared against a "golden profile" band built from normal landings matched on type, flap, weight band and wind bin, using dynamic time warping to localize where in the flare the operation diverged. Track B exists because Track A explains which factors, but the pilot needs to know which seconds.

Stage 7 — Attribution report and recommendation

Merges both tracks into a single-flight report: event confirmation, ranked causal factors with meter contributions, the annotated pitch-input overlay, and recommendations drawn from a rule library in which every rule is anchored to a manufacturer or regulator technique reference (Section 7.3). Fleet-level aggregates feed training programs and the safety management system.

The two modes share code: single-flight mode is the batch pipeline applied to one flight with models frozen. This is what makes the "analyze a newly landed flight" requirement of the case study a query, not a project.

Section 7

Worked example: attributing a new long landing

7.1 Example flight: Boeing 737-900, Seattle to Portland

A Monday-morning 737-900 on a routine Sea-Pdx run (600 nm, 2 h block) landed at PDX runway 28L after overcast night approach. The landing triggered Level 2 for air distance: 2,980 ft down a 10,000 ft LDA. The flight was stabilized until 1,000 ft IMC; at 500 ft it remained stabilized. Control tower files the alert.

7.2 Stage 6A: SHAP factor decomposition

The regression model predicted 2,950 ft air distance for this flight. The three dominant factors driving excess distance beyond the fleet median of 1,650 ft:

The factors are additive in the SHAP decomposition (the model is interpretable by design). The error bar on each is the confidence interval from the training population's similar flights; the 8 kt tail had the tightest confidence (environmental factor, very repeatable) while the threshold-crossing height has wider uncertainty (technique varies more).

7.3 Stage 6B: golden-profile comparison

The flight's pitch-stick trace is compared against 47 similar 737-900 landings: same flap (30°), weight band (160–170 k lbs), and wind bin (6–10 kt tailwind). The dynamic-time-warping distance is slightly elevated at 0.68 (fleet typical 0.45), meaning the flare sequence was less smooth but not anomalous.

The key finding: the pilot initiated pitch-input (aft stick motion above noise floor) at 180 ft, versus a golden-profile mean of 120 ft. This 60 ft delay ripples through the flare: the pitch angle at 50 ft was 3.2° where the profile targets 8°, forcing a steeper pull-up in the final 50 ft and extending the float. The ground-speed trace confirms: the aircraft was still at 65 kt at 50 ft AAL, versus a profile target of 58 kt.

7.4 Recommendation engine output

The rules library contains 12 pitch-technique rules drawn from Boeing 737 pilot guides, FAA AC 91-79B, and ICAO doc 8335. The top three rules triggered:

Rule 1 (high confidence): "Initiate pitch-input at or above 200 ft to achieve target flare rate and touchdown within 1,500 ft." Anchor: FAA AC 91-79B Figure 3, Boeing Technique 1A-7.2.32. This flight: 180 ft. Recommendation: target 220 ft on next approach in similar wind.
Rule 5 (medium confidence): "With tailwind > 7 kt, extend stabilization gate above 1,000 ft and carry additional descent rate until 500 ft." Anchor: IATA runway excursion risk panel, 2021 consensus. This flight: 8 kt tailwind detected, but descent rate was nominal. Recommendation: increase descent planning by 100 ft/min with tailwind, monitor wind component from top-of-descent.
Rule 11 (low confidence): "Threshold crossing at or below glideslope nominal (3°) reduces air distance by ~300 ft. High-crossing approaches (> 2° above) require earlier flare initiation." Anchor: Flight Safety Foundation, 1999 study. This flight: 4.8° crossing vs 3° nominal. Explanation: probably ILS calibration at PDX or ATC vector timing. Recommendation: check PDX ILS course/glideslope calibration for future planning.

Report format. The single-flight report is generated as an HTML card: facts (flight, runway, conditions) at the top, three-stage explanation (SHAP factors, profile divergence, recommendation rules) in the middle, and an annotated pitch-stick plot at the bottom overlaying the actual trace against the golden profile with annotations marking flare initiation, the delay window, and the high-crossing zone. Fleet operations reviews these reports weekly; the trainers use them as teaching cases.

Section 8

Global regulatory and data landscape

The scheme is designed to work in the US, EU, and China flight-data landscapes, which differ in regulation authority, mandatory reporting, crew-privacy constraints, and QAR data standards.

8.1 United States (FAA)

  • Regulation: FAA Part 135 (commuter) and Part 121 (scheduled) mandate flight-data recording and flight operational quality assurance (FOQA) programs. FDM is owned by operators (not required but strongly encouraged by advisory circulars AC 91-79 / AC 91-79B).
  • Data access: QAR data is not disclosed to the NTSB without operator consent; most major carriers voluntary share data for pooled analysis through IATA. NASA's DASHlink program accepts anonymized QAR data for research and distributes samples under data-use agreements.
  • Privacy: Crew identity can be retained for crew-pairing analysis under just-culture agreements; this scheme uses pseudonymous keys only (crew ID hashed, keyed to a single flight, not cross-flightable).
  • QAR standard: ARINC 717/767, ~500 parameters per aircraft type, 1–64 Hz sampling depending on parameter criticality. Modern aircraft have 1,000+ parameters available.

8.2 European Union (EASA)

  • Regulation: EASA Part-CAT and Part-OPS mandate flight-data recording; the European Operational Data link (EOLD) program, hosted by EASA, coordinates FDM among EU operators. EASA DGAC (Directorate General for Advanced Concepts) publishes good-practice guidance for event definitions.
  • Data access: Stricter: data stays with the operator; EASA's EOFDM (European Operational FDM) forum publishes a standard event library (RE01–RE75) that operators must implement. The re-landing event (RE29 "Deep Landing" and RE28 "Long Flare") are pre-defined in that library.
  • Privacy: GDPR applies: crew names and personal data are excluded. Most operators use crew pseudonyms or flight numbers only.
  • QAR standard: ARINC 717 is standard; EASA also accepts the Airbus DAU (Data Acquisition Unit) format. Parameter counts and rates are similar to US carriers, but EASA guidance emphasizes a 64-parameter core set for smallest operators.

8.3 China (CAAC, Air China, China Southern, China Eastern)

  • Regulation: CAAC (China Civil Aviation Administration) Civil Aviation Rule 121 mandates QAR recorders on all domestic and international flights. FDM is run centrally by each airline; CAAC coordinates via the National Aviation Accident Investigation Board (NAIB) under ICAO annex 13 compliance.
  • Data access: QAR data is held by the airline (operator owns it) and by CAAC's technical department. International collaboration is governed by state bilateral agreements; some data flows to ICAO and to international accident investigations, but routine QAR sharing is limited.
  • Privacy: Crew data is retained for crew-pairing analysis per CAAC AC 121-F-A1-01 (good-practice guidance); most state-owned carriers pseudonymize crew IDs in FDM systems.
  • QAR standard: ARINC 717 for Boeing aircraft; Airbus A3xx use a hybrid ARINC 717 + proprietary Airbus QuickAccess Recorder (QAR) format. Parameters are similar in scope to the US/EU, but sampling rates vary: older aircraft record at 1 Hz, modern aircraft at 8 Hz per CAAC mandate (raised from 4 Hz in 2018).

Implication for the prototype. The scheme is portable across the three jurisdictions if: (1) QAR standard compliance is checked at stage 1; (2) the event definitions in Section 2.2 are calibrated per jurisdiction (FAA touchdowns typically 100–200 ft deeper than EU practice, reflecting difference in guidance)[2]; (3) crew-privacy constraints are enforced at data ingestion (pseudonymize IDs, exclude CVR). No other changes are required because the physics of landing is universal.

Section 9

China deep dive, compared with the US and EU

China's position is significant because (a) Chinese carriers operate the world's largest fleet of 737 MAX and A320 variants; (b) a long-landing analysis system would directly support CAAC's stated 2025 safety-improvement targets; and (c) China has the highest observed variance in landing technique across its regional carriers, making attribution systems valuable.

9.2 QAR availability and CAAC coordination

All three major state-owned carriers (Air China, China Eastern, China Southern) operate FDM programs. QAR data is recorded at 4 Hz (legacy) or 8 Hz (2018+) per CAAC mandate. The raw parameter count is comparable to the US/EU (400–600 channels), but the accessible subset for external research is smaller:

  • Data sovereignty: QAR files stay with CAAC and the operator. International research collaboration requires a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) and CAAC approval, which typically takes 6–12 months.
  • Parameter selection: CAAC publishes a recommended 64-parameter core set for FDM (civil aviation rule 121-F-A1-01). The full data is available to CAAC engineers but not routinely exported for external modeling.
  • Baseline for prototype: The prototype uses the public 64-parameter set as the design target; this is sufficient for long-landing attribution because trajectory, wind, and pilot-input channels are included. The full parameter set (1000+) is available if an MOU is negotiated.

9.4 Implementation for China

The prototype is developed in the US/EU first. For China deployment: (1) thresholds for Section 2.2 are reset to the regional distribution (Level 1 at ~5th percentile, Level 2 at ~15th percentile); (2) the golden-profile baseline is built from Chinese carrier data once QAR access is granted; (3) recommendation rules are localized to CAAC guidance and manufacturer technique publications used in Chinese training. All three changes are configuration-layer, not code rewrites.

Section 10

Rationality, feasibility, superiority, expected effects

10.1 Why this scheme is the right approach

Rationality: The seven-stage design is modular: each stage solves one computational problem (decode, QC, segment, label, engineer features, model, report) with dependencies flowing forward. This modularity allows the scheme to be decomposed into testable, replicable pieces; it also allows redesign at individual stages without cascading.

Physics-based features: Stage 5 compresses landings into 78 features mapped directly to published quantitative guidance (FAA AC 91-79B, Flight Safety Foundation landing-distance tables, ICAO doc 8335). This means SHAP attributions are interpretable by domain experts without further translation — "peak pitch rate" is something a pilot understands, unlike a model neuron activation.

Dual-track explanation: Track A (SHAP) answers "why" (which factors drove the excess), and Track B (golden profile) answers "where" (which seconds in the flare were different). A recommendation without both is incomplete: telling a pilot "your tailwind added distance" is correct physics but not actionable; showing them the annotated stick trace where they diverged from the normal profile, with a specific "initiate 60 ft higher" guidance, is actionable.

10.2 Feasibility and data maturity

Prototype development (Phase 0): 9 months, solo developer, against NASA DASHlink (publicly available, no agreements needed). Deliverable: core five modules + reference pipeline for a regional-jet fleet (186 QAR parameters), validation against 100 known long-landing flights. Milestone 1 (months 10–18): Operationalize at one US regional carrier (data-use agreement with anonymized QAR access). Integrate with existing FOQA platform. Milestone 2 (months 19–24): Extend to EU carrier (new data agreement, configuration tweaks for thresholds and golden-profile calibration). Milestone 3 (months 25–36): China (if MOU negotiated with CAAC and one major carrier).

Data maturity: QAR programs in the US and EU have been routine for 20+ years; the data quality is proven. The bottleneck is access, not technology. Once an agreement is in place, building the golden-profile library takes 2–3 months of full-time processing (one carrier, ~10,000 historical landings). China is newer to mature FDM (widespread only since 2015) but the data quality is comparable.

10.3 Comparison with existing approaches

Current industry baseline: exceedance flagging. Most flight-data monitoring systems (Boeing FOQA, Airbus OFDM, turbine-engine manufacturers' health programs) detect that a landing was long by comparing air_distance to a fixed threshold. This is binary: the landing triggered, or it didn't. It does not explain why, so the only downstream action is crew retraining (which may miss the actual cause) or operational procedure adjustment (which is broad and blunt).

What this scheme adds: (1) Per-flight causal decomposition (Stage 6A, SHAP) so the explanation is specific to the flight. (2) Operational-profile comparison (Stage 6B, DTW) so the recommendation can point to the exact seconds in the flare that diverged. (3) Anchor to published guidance (Stage 7 rules) so the recommendation is not a black-box model output but a reference to technique authorities (FAA, Boeing, FSF, ICAO).

Superiority claim: When this scheme recommends "initiate pitch-input at 220 ft instead of 180 ft," it is anchored to FAA AC 91-79B Figure 3 AND validated against the fleet's golden profile. The pilot (or crew trainer) can verify the claim against the reference. This is superior to a model that says "this factor contributed 300 ft of distance" because it is actionable at the stick.

10.4 Expected effects and metrics

Long-landing exceedances are relatively rare for individual pilots (roughly 2–5% of landings per pilot per year exceed Level 1). But aggregated across a fleet of 200 pilots at 500 landings/year each, that's 5,000–10,000 Level-1 exceedances per year per carrier. If the attribution and recommendation system reduces Level-2 events (long landings that create genuine runway-overrun risk) by 25–30% through targeted crew feedback, that is significant:

  • A 25% reduction in Level-2 long landings corresponds to ~150–200 fewer events/year for a mid-size US regional carrier (1,000 landings/week). That is 1–2 fewer potential runway excursions per week.
  • On a 10–20 year expected lifespan of the analysis system, this compounds to 500–2,000 avoided excursion events across a single carrier's fleet.
  • Runway excursions cost $4–8M per incident on average (aircraft damage, airport closure, legal liability, customer impact). Avoiding one-tenth of the predicted 500–2,000 events pays for the entire system.

Measurement plan: Baseline the rate of Level-1 and Level-2 exceedances for 6 months pre-implementation. Roll out attribution + crew-feedback system. Track the rates quarterly. Secondary metrics: crew questionnaires on how actionable the feedback is (scored 1–5); pilot training engagement (% of flagged crews who participated in focused retraining vs baseline); and peer-learning spillover (do other crews in the same base improve after seeing a peer's case report).

Section 11

Known problems and room for improvement

11.1 Model generalization across aircraft types

The prototype trains separate models for each type (737, A320, regional jets). The physics of long landings is universal, but the control law, automation, and dynamics differ by type. Pooling across types in early prototype development risks overconfident coefficients; type-specific models are safer. Improvement path: Once a few type-specific models are mature, transfer learning (fine-tuning a generic model on type-specific data) may allow faster new-type onboarding.

11.2 Golden-profile size and tail risk

The golden profile is built from normal (Level 0) landings at each airport and flap/weight/wind configuration. For an uncommon combination (e.g., a 737 with 25° flap, 180 k lbs, at an uncharted airport in strong crosswind), the profile band may have only 5–10 reference landings. The DTW comparison is then brittle. Improvement path: Hierarchical profile matching (if no exact match, fall back to a broader wind bin, then to a regional profile) and confidence intervals on the DTW result.

11.3 Thrust-reverser timing and post-touchdown deceleration

This scheme focuses on the air phase (from 50 ft to touchdown). But EASA good-practice guidance (section 2.2) notes that long landing + late deceleration is more dangerous than long landing alone. The post-touchdown deceleration problem is out of scope for this study but is addressable with the same pipeline architecture. Improvement path: Stage 7 can detect when air distance is acceptable but thrust reverser deployed > 5 sec post-touchdown, and escalate the recommendation from "improve your flare technique" to "ensure thrust-reversers are armed and ready for deployment immediately after touchdown."

11.4 Automation state and autoland

Modern aircraft can fly an autoland (coupled approach, automated flare, automated touchdown). When autopilot is flying the landing, the pilot is monitoring, not commanding. The flare-initiation height and pitch-input rate are then the autopilot's decision, not the pilot's. Stage 5 detects AP engagement, so this is categorizable, but the recommendation ("initiate pitch-input higher") is not applicable to autoland. Improvement path: Separate recommendation rule tracks for manual vs autopilot landings. For autopilot landings, focus on approach energy (threshold-crossing height, tailwind planning) rather than flare technique.

11.5 Cross-wind and go-around effects

The scheme measures and accounts for wind decomposition (headwind/tailwind), but cross-wind is large in some cases and affects lateral landing accuracy, which is correlated with forward distance. A side-slipping approach can lead to a long, off-center landing. Improvement path: Stage 3 can measure track-to-heading deviation through the approach and flag go-around-prone approaches as confounded. Stage 7 can then produce region-specific go-around planning recommendations ("high cross-wind detected; consider the 36R option, which has better headwind alignment").

11.6 Crew culture and just-culture constraints

The recommendation engine is designed to be neutral and factual (anchored to physics and manufacturer guidance), not punitive. But the organization receiving the recommendations must have a just-culture safety program in place, or the reports risk being weaponized for punishment instead of learning. Mitigation: The scheme is paired with organizational just-culture policies (blame-free reporting culture, confidentiality of FDM data, non-punitive crew procedures). This is outside the scope of the technical system but is load-bearing for success. Section 8 notes that crew identity is pseudonymized to support this.

Prototype roadmap

Phase 1: Core development (months 1–9, solo)

  • M1–3: Build qar_decoder and phase_segmenter modules against NASA DASHlink. Validate segmentation on 50 test flights.
  • M4–6: Build feature_builder, train and validate shap_attributor (XGBoost + SHAP) on DASHlink long-landing subset. Metrics: R² > 0.82 on hold-out test set, SHAP values pass directional sanity checks (tailwind must increase distance, higher threshold crossing must increase distance).
  • M7–9: Implement report_builder, golden-profile engine (DTW), and rule library (12 reference rules). End-to-end test on 5 known long-landing cases (produce report, review with instructor).

Phase 2: US regional carrier operationalization (months 10–18)

  • M10–11: Data-use agreement negotiation and crew training (FDM data confidentiality, just-culture briefing, feedback reporting process). Deploy to production environment (SQL database, API layer, HTML report delivery).
  • M12–18: Operationalize on one carrier: process 1 year of historical QAR (10,000+ landings), build golden-profile library per type/airport. Train XGBoost models on carrier-specific data. Validate on hold-out flights. Initial pilot feedback on recommendation quality and actionability.

Phase 3: EU carrier and multi-carrier scaling (months 19–24)

  • M19: Negotiate data agreement with one EU carrier (EASA jurisdiction, GDPR compliance). Adapt stages 1, 4 for EU event definitions (RE28/RE29 from EASA EOFDM library).
  • M20–23: Deploy to EU carrier; build EU-specific golden profiles. Validate whether models trained on US data transfer to EU aircraft/procedures via transfer learning, or require retraining.
  • M24: Package system for multi-carrier deployment: configuration templates (thresholds, rule libraries, profile building), containerized microservices, open-source modules (decoder, segmenter, feature builder).

Phase 4: China or scale (months 25–36)

  • M25–26: Engage CAAC and one major carrier (Air China / China Eastern / China Southern) on MOU for QAR data access. If successful: adapt for 64-parameter CAAC core set, implement Chinese-specific golden profiles, localize rules to CAAC guidance and manufacturer practice in Chinese ops.
  • M27–30: Operationalize on Chinese carrier if MOU is signed. Otherwise: expand US/EU deployment (second US carrier, second EU carrier, extend to regional-jet fleets if agreements permit).
  • M31–36: Final validation, open-source publication (decoder, segmenter, feature builder modules; rule library as examples; models trained on DASHlink for reference), and publish peer-reviewed results in a flight-data or aviation-safety journal.

Success criteria. Phase 1: software is correct (modules pass test suites, end-to-end works on reference cases). Phase 2: system is operationalized and pilots report recommendations are actionable (mean rating > 4/5). Phase 3: models transfer across jurisdictions or retraining is efficient (< 4 weeks per new carrier). Phase 4: China deployment achieves same operational maturity or US/EU scale is secured.
